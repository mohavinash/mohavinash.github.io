# History of Delimitation Amendments in India

## The Freeze Chain

| Year | Amendment | What It Did | Census Used |
|------|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| 1976 | 42nd Amendment | Froze seat allocation at 1971 levels until "first census after year 2000" | 1971 (frozen) |
| 2001 | 84th Amendment | Extended freeze: substituted "2000" with "2026" directly | 1971 (frozen) |
| 2003 | 87th Amendment | Partial unlock: allowed internal constituency boundaries to be redrawn using 2001 census. Total seats per state unchanged. | 2001 (boundaries only) |
| 2023 | 106th Amendment | Women's 33% reservation — but tied to next delimitation after a new census | N/A (dormant) |
| 2026 | 131st Amendment Bill | Deletes the freeze entirely, delegates census choice to Parliament by ordinary law | TBD by Parliament |

## Key Historical Fact (Verified)

The 84th Amendment (2001) jumped directly from "after 2000" to "after 2026." There was **no intermediate step** and **no COVID-related extension**. The freeze to "after 2026" has been in constitutional text since 2001.

**Actual text of 84th Amendment:**
> In article 81... for the figures "2000", the figures "2026" shall be substituted.
> In article 82... for the figures "2000", the figures "2026" shall be substituted.

**Source:** Election Commission of India, [84th Amendment text](https://www.eci.gov.in/delimitation); Lawsisto constitutional database

## Delimitation Commissions

| Commission | Period | Census Used | Key Action |
|------------|--------|-------------|------------|
| 1st | 1952–55 | 1951 | First delimitation: 489 Lok Sabha seats |
| 2nd | 1962–66 | 1961 | Increased to 522 seats |
| 3rd | 1972–75 | 1971 | Reached 543 seats. Overtaken by 42nd Amendment freeze |
| 4th | 2002–08 | 2001 | Redrew internal boundaries only (per 87th Amendment). Did NOT change total seats per state. Chaired by Justice Kuldip Singh |

## 42nd Amendment Rationale (1976)

During the Emergency, Indira Gandhi's government froze seats because southern states that had adopted family planning would lose seats to faster-growing northern states — creating a perverse incentive against population control.

## 84th Amendment Rationale (2001)

The freeze was extended because the demographic divergence had only intensified. Southern states (Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh) had dramatically slowed population growth while UP, Bihar, MP, Rajasthan had not. Unfreezing would have massively redistributed power northward.
